## Introduction

Every smartphone contains a suite of sensors — accelerometer, gyroscope, GPS, barometer, and more — that can capture rich data about movement, location, and context. In this tutorial you will use your phone as a sensing device, streaming data directly into Data Foundry without any hardware setup or programming.

The vehicle is **Sensor Logger**, a free app that reads your phone's built-in sensors and pushes measurements to a URL at a configurable interval. Data Foundry's **Data Logger** tool generates that URL automatically and stores everything it receives in an IoT dataset you can inspect in real time.

You will conduct a short data collection session — a 10-minute walk — and then examine what the time-series data reveals: what movement looks like as numbers, how sampling rate shapes what you can see, and what the data cannot tell you.

By the end you will have:
- A live IoT dataset populated with accelerometer and GPS readings from your own body
- A first-hand sense of the design decisions that shape sensor studies: sensor selection, sampling rate, session duration
- A Starboard notebook that profiles and visualises the collected time series

## Before you start

### Install Sensor Logger

Download **Sensor Logger** on your smartphone:

- **iOS:** [App Store](https://apps.apple.com/app/id1531582925)
- **Android:** [Google Play](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.kelvin.sensorapp)

Open the app once to grant the sensor and location permissions it requests. You do not need to create an account.

### Set up your Data Foundry project

1. Go to [Data Foundry](https://datafoundry.io.tudelft.nl/) and create a project (or open an existing one). Name it something like `Movement Study`.
2. In the **General** tab, add an **IoT Dataset** — this will store every sensor reading. Name it `Phone Sensors`. Note the numeric **dataset ID** shown in the top-left corner of the dataset page.
3. In the **Sources** tab, add a device. Name it something that identifies your phone (e.g. `my-phone`). Once created, note the device **reference ID** (a short alphanumeric string).

{: .note }
> The IoT Dataset stores time series data: one row per measurement, with a timestamp and a JSON blob of sensor values. Every push from Sensor Logger becomes a row.

---

## Step 1 — Generate the Sensor Logger URL

Data Foundry's **Data Logger** tool builds the endpoint URL that Sensor Logger will push data to.

1. In Data Foundry, go to **Data tools → Data Logger**.
2. Under **1. Select a project and dataset**, choose `Movement Study` and then `Phone Sensors`.
3. Under **2. Select a device**, choose `my-phone` (or whichever device you created).
4. The tool now shows two options. Find the **Sensor Logger** section (right panel) and copy the URL shown there. It looks like:

   ```
   https://datafoundry.io.tudelft.nl/tools/datalogger/{dataset-id}/{dataset-token}/{device-id}
   ```

   Keep this URL to hand — you will paste it into Sensor Logger in the next step.

{: .note }
> The URL encodes your dataset ID, a secret API token, and your device ID. Anyone with this URL can push data to your dataset. Treat it like a password: do not share it publicly or commit it to version control.

---

## Step 2 — Configure Sensor Logger

Open Sensor Logger on your phone and configure it to push data to Data Foundry.

### Connect to Data Foundry

1. On the main screen, tap the **gear icon** to open settings.
2. Find **Push URL** (or **HTTP Push**) and paste the URL you copied in Step 1.
3. Set the **Push interval** to **1 second**. (You will experiment with this later.)
4. Make sure **Push to server** is enabled.

### Select your sensors

For this tutorial, start with two sensor types to keep the data manageable:

- **Accelerometer** — measures linear acceleration in three axes (X, Y, Z), in m/s². This captures how the phone moves and shakes as you walk.
- **Location** — records GPS latitude, longitude, altitude, speed, and accuracy. This traces where you went.

Disable all other sensors for now. You can re-enable them later once you have explored this first dataset.

{: .note }
> The fewer sensors you enable, the smaller each JSON payload and the less battery the app consumes. For longer deployments, sensor selection is a real design decision — not just a tutorial simplification.

---

## Step 3 — Collect a walking session

You are going to conduct a short, deliberate data collection session. The goal is to generate data that has some structure you can later reason about — not just random movement.

### Plan a route with phases

Design a 10-minute route that includes two or three recognisably different movement modes:

- **Sitting still** for 1 minute before you leave (baseline)
- **Walking at a normal pace** for 5 minutes
- **Standing still** for 1 minute (e.g., waiting at a crossing)
- **Walking again** for 3 minutes back

Note the approximate start time of each phase — you will use these timestamps when you analyse the data.

### Start logging

1. On the Sensor Logger main screen, tap the **Start Recording button** to start logging.
2. You should see sensor values updating in real time on screen.
3. Go back to Data Foundry in a browser (on your laptop or another device) and open your `Phone Sensors` dataset. Click **Data Table**. You should see rows appearing as Sensor Logger pushes each batch.

   If no rows appear after 10 seconds, double-check the Push URL and that the app is connected to a network.

4. Walk your planned route. Sensor Logger will push a batch every second.
5. When you return, tap the **End Recording button** to end the session.

{: .note }
> GPS can take 15–30 seconds to acquire a signal after you step outside. Rows during this warmup period may show `null` or `0` for latitude and longitude. This is normal — plan your route so the first minute of walking is after you already have a signal.

## Step 4 — Inspect the data in Data Foundry

### Data Table

Go to your `Phone Sensors` dataset and click **Data Table**. You will see one row per second (or per batch, depending on your push interval). Each row has:

- A **timestamp** marked as 'ts' (when Data Foundry received the batch)
- A column for each sensor value pushed at that moment. The GPS data include latitude, longitude but also altitude and other information. The accelerometer data include x, y and z.

Look at a few rows and answer:

- Which sensor channels appear in the data? Are they consistent across rows?
- What values does the accelerometer report when you were standing still versus walking?
- Are there any rows with missing GPS fields? Where in the session did those appear?

### Built-in visualisation

Click **Data Visualisation** on the dataset page. Toggle on the accelerometer axes (`x`, `y`, `z`) and observe the time series. You should be able to identify the sitting, walking, and standing phases from the amplitude of the signal.

Try selecting a shorter time window covering just one walking phase. Does the pattern change?

## Step 5 — Analyse the time series in a Starboard notebook

### Set up a notebook

1. In Data Foundry, add an **Existing Dataset** to your project. Name it `Analysis Notebook`.
2. Go to **Data tools → Notebooks**, click **Add a New Notebook**, and choose the `Analysis Notebook` dataset.
3. Open the notebook. Delete the default JavaScript cell.

### Download or get the link to your data as CSV

On the `Phone Sensors` dataset page, find the Configuration tab **CSV/JSON DOWNLOAD** and generate a token link. Copy the token link for the CSV download. You can also download this CSV file via the Download button on the top right corner. However, with this link we can directly load our data in a Starboard notebook.

Back to your Starboard notebook, you can use the following code, replacing 'your-dataset-link'

{: .note }
> Regularly go to the top of the notebook and click **Save notebook** to save a copy on Data Foundry. Starboard Notebooks live in your browser — nothing is saved automatically.

### Load and inspect the data

```python
import pandas as pd
import io
from pyodide.http import pyfetch

DATASET_LINK = "your-dataset-link"

resp = await pyfetch(DATASET_LINK)
data = await resp.string()
df = pd.read_csv(io.StringIO(data))

print("Shape:", df.shape)
print("Columns:", df.columns.tolist())
df.head()
```

The CSV is already flat — one column per sensor field, one row per measurement. The accelerometer values are in columns `x`, `y`, `z`; GPS fields include `latitude`, `longitude`, `speed`, and `altitude`. Most rows are **sparse**: a row produced by an accelerometer update has empty GPS columns, and vice versa. This reflects the fact that different sensors update at different rates.

### Parse the timestamp and separate sensor streams

```python
# Convert the ts column to a proper datetime
df["ts"] = pd.to_datetime(df["ts"])
df = df.sort_values("ts").reset_index(drop=True)

print("Session start:", df["ts"].min())
print("Session end:  ", df["ts"].max())
print("Duration:     ", df["ts"].max() - df["ts"].min())

# Split into separate DataFrames — one per sensor stream
df_accel = df.dropna(subset=["x"]).copy()   # rows with accelerometer data
df_gps   = df.dropna(subset=["latitude"]).copy()  # rows with GPS fix

print(f"\nAccelerometer rows: {len(df_accel)}")
print(f"GPS rows:           {len(df_gps)}")
```

### Compute movement intensity

The three accelerometer axes each measure one direction of acceleration. Combining them gives a single **magnitude** — a rough index of overall movement intensity.

```python
import numpy as np

# Euclidean magnitude of the acceleration vector
# At rest: magnitude ≈ 9.8 m/s² (gravity alone, no movement)
# Walking: the signal oscillates above and below that baseline with each step
df_accel["magnitude"] = np.sqrt(
    df_accel["x"]**2 +
    df_accel["y"]**2 +
    df_accel["z"]**2
)

print("Acceleration magnitude stats:")
print(df_accel["magnitude"].describe().round(2))
```

### Profile movement phases

Using the timestamps you noted during collection, assign a label to each phase.

```python
# Replace these with your actual session start time and phase boundaries.
# Read the timestamps from the df_accel output above — copy the exact values.
# The ts column has no timezone info, so use tz=None here.
walk1_start = pd.Timestamp("2025-04-14 10:01:00")
stand_start = pd.Timestamp("2025-04-14 10:06:00")
walk2_start = pd.Timestamp("2025-04-14 10:07:00")
session_end = pd.Timestamp("2025-04-14 10:10:00")

def label_phase(ts):
    if ts < walk1_start:
        return "sitting"
    elif ts < stand_start:
        return "walking-1"
    elif ts < walk2_start:
        return "standing"
    elif ts <= session_end:
        return "walking-2"
    else:
        return "other"

df_accel["phase"] = df_accel["ts"].apply(label_phase)

# Summary statistics per phase
df_accel.groupby("phase")["magnitude"].agg(
    count = "count",
    mean  = "mean",
    std   = "std",
    max   = "max"
).round(2)
```

Expected output (approximate values — yours will differ):

```
           count   mean   std    max
phase
other          5   9.81  0.09  10.03
sitting       58   9.83  0.12  10.21
standing      61   9.89  0.18  10.54
walking-1    298  10.71  1.34  15.88
walking-2    182  10.65  1.28  15.32
```

Notice that:
- **Sitting and standing** both show magnitude ≈ 9.8 m/s² (gravity alone, minimal movement)
- **Walking** phases show a higher mean and much higher standard deviation — each step creates a burst of acceleration

### Check GPS coverage

```python
# How many rows have a GPS fix vs accelerometer readings?
print(f"Total rows:             {len(df)}")
print(f"Accelerometer rows:     {len(df_accel)}")
print(f"GPS rows:               {len(df_gps)}")

# Speed statistics (m/s) — gives a rough sense of pace
print(f"\nSpeed (m/s): mean={df_gps['speed'].mean():.2f}, max={df_gps['speed'].max():.2f}")
# Walking is typically 1.2–1.6 m/s; running > 2.5 m/s

# Area covered: rough bounding box from lat/lon range
# 0.0001° latitude ≈ 11 m; longitude varies by latitude
lat_range = df_gps["latitude"].max() - df_gps["latitude"].min()
lon_range = df_gps["longitude"].max() - df_gps["longitude"].min()
print(f"\nLatitude range:  {lat_range:.5f}°  (~{lat_range * 111_000:.0f} m)")
print(f"Longitude range: {lon_range:.5f}°")
```

### Experiment: resample at a lower rate

Part of your data collection involved choosing a 1-second push interval. Now simulate what the data would look like at a coarser rate.

```python
# Resample the accelerometer stream to 10-second averages
df_10s = (
    df_accel
    .set_index("ts")
    .resample("10s")["magnitude"]
    .agg(["mean", "std", "count"])
    .rename(columns={"mean": "magnitude_mean", "std": "magnitude_std", "count": "n_samples"})
    .reset_index()
)

print(f"At 1 s:  {len(df_accel)} rows")
print(f"At 10 s: {len(df_10s)} rows")
print("\nFirst 10 rows at 10-second resolution:")
print(df_10s.head(10).round(3))
```

Compare the two resolutions:
- At 1 second, individual footsteps are visible as spikes in the magnitude signal.
- At 10 seconds, you see the average activity level per window — individual steps disappear into the mean.
- Which resolution would you want for a study lasting one week? One month?

## Reflect

Before using this approach in a design project, consider:

1. **What the sensor measures vs what you want to know.** Accelerometer magnitude tracks phone movement, not step count, not effort, not stress. GPS records a trace, not purpose or experience. What additional data — contextual, self-reported, or from another sensor — would you need to answer a design research question like *"when do people feel unsafe walking in this neighbourhood"*?

2. **Sampling rate as a design choice.** You collected at 1 second. For a step detection algorithm, this is barely sufficient — biomechanics research uses 50–200 Hz. For a week-long home energy study, 1 Hz would generate 600,000 rows per sensor. What is the minimum sampling rate that would answer your specific research question, and what does that choice cost in storage, battery, and participant burden?

3. **The phone as a sensor platform.** You wore (or carried) the phone in your pocket or hand. That placement changes the accelerometer orientation on every step and every time you switch hands. Professional wrist-worn or chest-worn sensors have fixed orientations. How does sensor placement affect the validity of your data, and how would you need to account for it in analysis?

4. **Consent and transparency.** Sensor Logger collected your location at 1-second resolution for 10 minutes. You consented to this because it was your own study. Now imagine asking a participant to carry a logging phone for two weeks during their home routine. What would you need to disclose, and how would you design opt-out or pause mechanisms?

5. **Missing data and deployment failure.** Sensors fail. GPS loses signal indoors. Phones run out of battery. Networks disconnect. In your 10-minute session, how much data was missing or unreliable? How would you plan for this in a study that runs unsupervised for days?
