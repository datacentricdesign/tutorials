## Introduction

In this tutorial you will design, deploy, and analyse a short Experience Sampling study using **m-Path**, a smartphone-based research platform widely used for ambulatory assessment and intensive longitudinal data collection. You will be both researcher and participant: you will design a probe in the m-Path dashboard, schedule prompts to your own phone, collect responses over two days, and then analyse the exported data in Python.

The aim is hands-on experience with the decisions the textbook describes: what questions to ask, which prompting strategy to use, how participant burden feels from the inside, and what self-report data looks like as a time series. You will also reach the natural limits of the built-in dashboard, and then move beyond them with a raw CSV export.

By the end you will have:
- A small ESM dataset collected from your own daily experience
- A first-hand sense of how prompting strategy shapes what you capture — and what it costs participants
- A Python script that loads, profiles, and visualises ESM time-series data

## Step 1 — Create the questionnaire

Experience sampling probes work best when they are short enough to complete in under a minute. Five items is a practical upper bound for a prompt that will appear several times a day.

Design a probe that captures **affect**, **activity context**, and one **open reflection**. The following items are a good starting point:

| # | Question | Type | Range / Options |
|---|----------|------|-----------------|
| 1 | How do you feel right now? | Visual Analogue Scale | 0 (very bad) — 10 (very good) |
| 2 | How energised do you feel? | Visual Analogue Scale | 0 (exhausted) — 10 (energised) |
| 3 | What were you doing just now? | Multiple Choice | Working / Studying / Exercising / Commuting / Socialising / Eating / Resting / Other |
| 4 | Are you alone or with others? | Multiple Choice | Alone / With one or two people / In a group |
| 5 | Anything you want to note about this moment? | Open-Ended Text | (optional) |

### Build the interaction in the dashboard

1. Open the **Participant** tab. You should see yourself listed there (highlighted in orange).
2. Click the **pen and paper icon** next to your name to open the questionnaire editor for this participant.
3. Click **Add a new interaction**, then select **Start from scratch**.
4. Click the icon next to **New Interaction** in the top green bar to rename the interaction (e.g. `Daily Experience`).
5. Add the five items one by one. For each, type the question text and press Enter. A settings popup appears with:
   - **Question field:** the text shown to the participant
   - **Label field:** a short internal identifier used in the data export — use `mood`, `energy`, `activity`, `social`, `note`
   - **Question type selector:** choose Visual Analogue Scale for sliders, Multiple Choice for option lists, Open-Ended Text for free text
6. For sliders (items 1 and 2), set the range to 0–10 and add anchor labels at each end.
7. For the free-text item (item 5), mark it as optional.
8. An **orange dot** in the top bar indicates unsaved changes. Press **Save all** when done.

{: .note }
> Keep the free-text item optional. Mandatory open questions reduce completion rates sharply: participants skip the whole prompt rather than type under time pressure.

### Test the questionnaire

Click **Test on me** to send the interaction to your phone immediately. Complete all five items as a participant would, and check:
- Do all question types render as expected?
- Are the slider anchors readable?
- Does the optional free-text item appear optional?

Iterate in the dashboard and re-test until the probe feels natural to complete quickly.

### Export to your library

Export the interaction so it can be reused for any future participant without rebuilding from scratch:

1. Click the export icon on the interaction.
2. Set sharing to **None** — this makes the interaction private, visible only in your own library.
3. Confirm. The interaction now appears in your library under its label.

If a partner has already connected to your account, open the **Participant** tab, select your partner, click the pen and paper icon, and retrieve the `Daily Experience` interaction from your library rather than starting from scratch.

## Step 2 — Schedule the prompts

This is where you operationalise the prompting strategies described in the textbook. You will give each day its own strategy: Day 1 uses a **fixed** schedule (prompts at predictable times) and Day 2 uses a **random** schedule (prompts at unpredictable times within the same windows). Running one strategy per day keeps the daily prompt count manageable while giving you a direct within-person comparison.

Navigate to your participant's **Schedule** tab. You see a calendar view. Click the **Fill** button to open the **Add to schedule** dialogue.

### Day 1 — Fixed schedule

In the **Add to schedule** dialogue:

1. Select the **Add from interaction** tab (the leftmost option).
2. Under **Interaction**, choose `Daily Experience`.
3. Under **Scheme**, select **5 notifications per day**. This distributes five prompts across the waking day at preset times.
4. Set **Starting from** to today's date and **Number of days** to **1**.
5. Check **Override scheme defaults** to reveal the randomisation option.
6. Under **Randomization scheme**, select **Fire at start time** — prompts fire at their scheduled time exactly.
7. Click **OK**.

Five time blocks appear on Day 1 of the calendar. Click any block to open its beep properties and verify that the `Daily Experience` interaction is attached. You can also set an **expiration** here (e.g. 30 minutes) so that missed prompts close automatically.

### Day 2 — Random schedule

Click **Fill** again to open the dialogue a second time:

1. Select the **Add from interaction** tab.
2. Under **Interaction**, choose `Daily Experience`.
3. Under **Scheme**, select **5 notifications per day** again — the same five time windows, but now prompts will fire at an unpredictable moment within each one.
4. Set **Starting from** to tomorrow's date and **Number of days** to **1**.
5. Check **Override scheme defaults**.
6. Under **Randomization scheme**, select **Fire randomly between start time and end time**.
7. Click **OK**.

Five blocks appear on Day 2. The scheduled times now represent windows rather than exact moments — each prompt fires at a random point within its window.

{: .note }
> Five prompts per day is within the 4–8 range the textbook identifies as typical for 1–2 week studies. Over just two days this is already enough to experience the difference between knowing when a prompt is coming and not knowing.

If you enrolled a partner, select their name in the **Participant** tab and repeat both Fill steps for their schedule.

## Step 3 — Collect (two-day pilot)

Let the prompts run for two full days. When a prompt arrives on your phone:

- Complete it immediately if circumstances allow. If the timing is genuinely impossible (e.g. you are driving or in a meeting), let the window expire.
- Do not go back to fill in missed prompts. Expired prompts produce naturally missing data, which is informative about burden.

Keep a brief private log — on paper or in a separate notes app — of:

- Prompts you skipped, and the reason
- Whether fixed and random prompts ever arrived close together in the same hour
- Any responses where your answer felt shaped by awareness of being observed rather than genuine state

You will return to this log in the Reflect section.

## Step 4 — Inspect the data in the dashboard

After two days, return to the researcher dashboard.

### Monitoring panel

The monitoring view shows participant-level progress: prompts delivered, prompts completed, and completion rate. Look at:

- Your overall completion rate. How does it compare across the fixed and random schedules?
- The distribution of response times within the 30-minute window — did you tend to respond immediately or at the last moment?

### Case-analysis view

Browse your own responses chronologically. m-Path's built-in view is useful for spotting individual anomalies and getting a quick sense of patterns:

- Do mood and energy scores tend to rise and fall together, or do they sometimes diverge?
- Are certain activity types associated with particular affect ratings?
- Do any free-text notes add context that the numerical items cannot capture?

The dashboard is a good starting point, but it limits how deeply you can compare variables, compute statistics, or align responses with external context. The next step moves beyond what the interface provides.

## Step 5 — Export and analyse

### Export your data

m-Path provides **per-participant data export** as a CSV file on the free plan.

Navigate to your participant's data view and download the CSV for `P01`. If you enrolled a partner, download their file separately. Each CSV contains one row per completed survey response, with columns for:

- Timestamps for prompt delivery and response submission
- One column per survey item, identified by the label you set (e.g. `mood`, `energy`)
- The beep label that triggered this prompt (`fixed` or `random`)

Save the file(s) to a local folder.

{: .note }
> Run `df.columns.tolist()` first to see the actual column names in your export — they reflect m-Path's internal naming and the labels you defined. Rename as needed in the step below.

### Load and inspect

```python
import pandas as pd

# Load the exported CSV — adjust the filename to match yours
df = pd.read_csv("P01_export.csv")

print("Shape:", df.shape)
print("\nColumns:")
print(df.columns.tolist())
df.head(3)
```

Look at the output and answer:
- How many rows does the dataset have? Does this match your expected number of completed responses?
- What format are the timestamps in? Are they in UTC or local time?
- Which columns correspond to the five survey items you designed?

### Parse timestamps and rename columns

```python
# Parse the timestamp of response submission — adjust the column name to match your export
df["submitted_at"] = pd.to_datetime(df["submitted_at"])
df = df.sort_values("submitted_at").reset_index(drop=True)

# Rename item columns to readable labels — adjust the left-hand keys to your actual column names
df = df.rename(columns={
    "item_mood":     "mood",
    "item_energy":   "energy",
    "item_activity": "activity",
    "item_social":   "social",
    "item_note":     "note",
})

print("Study period:")
print("  Start:    ", df["submitted_at"].min())
print("  End:      ", df["submitted_at"].max())
print("  Responses:", len(df))
```

### Compute completion rate

```python
# Total prompts delivered — read this from the monitoring panel in the dashboard
# For 7 prompts/day × 2 days, this should be 14; adjust if any failed to deliver
prompts_delivered = 14

completion_rate = len(df) / prompts_delivered * 100
print(f"Completion rate: {len(df)}/{prompts_delivered} ({completion_rate:.0f}%)")

# Completion rate by schedule label — adjust the column name to match your export
schedule_col = "label"

if schedule_col in df.columns:
    print("\nResponses by schedule:")
    print(df[schedule_col].value_counts())
```

### Plot mood and energy over time

```python
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(11, 4))

ax.plot(df["submitted_at"], df["mood"],   marker="o", label="Mood",   linewidth=1.5)
ax.plot(df["submitted_at"], df["energy"], marker="s", label="Energy", linewidth=1.5, linestyle="--")

ax.set_ylim(0, 10)
ax.set_xlabel("Time")
ax.set_ylabel("Rating (0–10)")
ax.set_title("Mood and energy over the study period")
ax.legend()
ax.tick_params(axis="x", rotation=45)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

Look at the plot:
- Are mood and energy correlated? When do they diverge?
- Can you identify peaks or dips that correspond to events you remember?
- Is there a visible time-of-day pattern (morning vs evening)?

### Compare mood by activity context

```python
# Mean and count of mood ratings per reported activity
mood_by_activity = (
    df.groupby("activity")["mood"]
    .agg(n="count", mean="mean", std="std")
    .sort_values("mean", ascending=False)
    .round(2)
)
print(mood_by_activity)
```

### Compare fixed vs random prompts

```python
if schedule_col in df.columns:
    print("Mean mood by schedule:")
    print(df.groupby(schedule_col)["mood"].mean().round(2))

    print("\nMean energy by schedule:")
    print(df.groupby(schedule_col)["energy"].mean().round(2))
```

Does the prompting strategy appear to affect ratings? Fixed prompts are predictable — participants may arrive mentally prepared. Random prompts catch whatever state is actually present. Whether this difference appears in your data is an empirical question.

## Reflect

Before designing a full ESM deployment, consider what this pilot revealed:

1. **Burden and compliance.** What was your completion rate? When did you skip prompts, and why? Seven prompts over a day may already feel intrusive. The textbook recommends 4–8 for studies lasting one to two weeks. After two days at seven, how does that scale feel sustainable?

2. **Fixed versus random.** You experienced both strategies first-hand. Did the predictability of the fixed prompts change how you prepared your answers? Did the random prompts catch you in contexts you would not otherwise have reported? Compare completion rates: which schedule had higher drop-off, and why?

3. **What self-report cannot capture.** Your dataset shows what you *reported* about your state, not necessarily your state itself. Looking at your private log: were there moments when fatigue, embarrassment, or awareness of the researcher's view shaped your answer? This is social desirability and recall bias in the data you collected.

4. **What sensors would add.** Your data has affect, activity context, and social setting — all self-reported. Now imagine overlaying accelerometer data from T3.3 for the same two days. Which reported activities would be confirmed by movement patterns? Which affect ratings correlate with physical activity? What questions become answerable that were not before? What questions remain beyond reach even with both data sources combined?

5. **Consent and passive prompting.** The random schedule delivered prompts without warning. For a two-day self-study, this felt low-stakes. How would you design the consent process for a two-week deployment with participants who experience mental health difficulties — where an intrusive prompt at the wrong moment could cause harm?
