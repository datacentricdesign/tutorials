# T4.2 · Exploring Visual Data

**Textbook context:** Video logging, thematic compilation, anonymisation, preprocessing for CV.
{: .text-grey-dk-000 }

**Data Foundry features:** Media Dataset, Annotation Dataset, Telegram bot  
**Documentation pages:** [Media Dataset](https://data-foundry.net/Learning/Datasets/DatasetsOverview/mediadataset.html) · [Annotation Dataset](https://data-foundry.net/Learning/Datasets/DatasetsOverview/annotationdataset.html) · [Add Participant](https://data-foundry.net/Guides/ProjectManagement/AddParticipant.html)

## Introduction

Design research regularly uses photography as a probe: participants photograph their environment in response to a brief, producing material that sensors cannot capture — the texture of a place, the moment something caught attention, the friction of a poorly designed interface in context. This technique, often called photovoice, combines structured prompting with participant-directed image-making.

In this tutorial you will run a small photovoice collection on yourself. You will write a brief design question, send it to yourself via the Data Foundry Telegram bot as if you were a participant receiving a researcher's prompt, go on a 15-minute walk and collect photographs via Telegram, and then apply the visual exploration workflow from the textbook to what you collected: anonymise, log, compile, and reflect.

Acting as both researcher and participant is deliberate. The DCD textbook (p. 323) notes that the camera's directedness encodes the researcher's intuitions about what matters — and that the same applies when the camera is in a participant's hands. By occupying both roles you will experience that dynamic first-hand: you will feel the difference between the prompt you wrote and the images it produced, and you will notice which moments you chose to capture and why.

By the end you will have:
- A live Media Dataset populated from your own Telegram-based walk session
- Practice applying anonymisation, video logging, and thematic compilation
- A candidate theme and a reflexivity note ready to bring into a participatory session (Chapter 5)

2. In the **General** tab, add a **Media Dataset**. Name it `Walk Photos`.
3. In the **General** tab, add an **Annotation Dataset**. Name it `Walk Log`. You will use this in Step 6.

### Add yourself as a participant

4. Go to the **Participants** tab of your project.
5. Click **Add participant** and enter your own email address. Data Foundry will create a participant record for you in this project.
6. On the participant page, you can open the participation link or send it by email. Note the **project participation code** shown when loading that link — a short alphanumeric string. You will enter this in Telegram to link your participant account to this project.

### Locate your personal PIN

7. In Data Foundry, go to your **Profile** page (top-right menu). Find the **Telegram PIN** — a five-digit number. You will use this when registering with the bot.

## Step 1 — Design your collection brief

Before opening Telegram, write the prompt that will guide what you photograph. A good photovoice brief is:

- **Focused enough** to produce coherent material — it gives you a reason to photograph some things and not others.
- **Open enough** to allow surprise — it should not pre-determine what you will find.
- **Grounded in a design question** — the images should be useful for reasoning about a product, service, or space.

A starting brief for this tutorial: *"As you walk, photograph aspects of your environment or routine that a connected product could usefully sense — and moments where collecting that data would feel intrusive or raise a question about consent."* This is tied to the design question: *where does the built environment offer data collection opportunities, and what are the limits of what should be sensed?*

You can use this brief as-is, or adapt it to a context that is more relevant to a project you are already working on — a specific campus building, a neighbourhood, a particular type of infrastructure.

Write your brief down. You will send it to yourself as a Telegram message in Step 4.

---

## Step 2 — Register with the Telegram bot as a participant

Open Telegram on your phone and find the Data Foundry bot. For the TU Delft instance, it is called `@datafoundrydelft`.

1. Open the bot and tap **Start** (or send `/start`).
2. The bot will ask whether you are a researcher or a participant. Reply `participant`.
3. Enter your **email address** when prompted.
4. Enter your **Telegram PIN** from your Data Foundry profile page when prompted.
5. The bot confirms your identity. It will now ask which project you want to join. Enter the **participation code** from the Participants tab of your project.
6. The bot confirms you are now a participant in the project and that you can send photos.

{: .note }
> If the bot says it cannot find your email, check that the email you enter matches the one registered in Data Foundry exactly. If it says photos are unavailable, check that the `Walk Photos` Media Dataset exists in the project and that you are registered as a participant in the correct project.

---

## Step 3 — Send yourself the collection brief

Switch to researcher mode. In Data Foundry:

1. Go to the **Participants** tab of your project.
2. Find the **Send Telegram message** button.
3. Choose **Message all participants** (or message yourself specifically).
4. Paste your collection brief from Step 1 into the message field, together with any logistical instructions: how long the walk should be, roughly how many photos to aim for, and what to do if a moment is hard to photograph (e.g., a voice note via `/diary` describing it instead).
5. Send the message.

Check your phone — you should receive the message from the bot as a participant would.

{: .note }
> This step makes visible a design decision in every participant study: the researcher's framing shapes what participants produce before they take a single photograph. Read the message you just received and ask yourself: what does it invite? What does it implicitly exclude?

---

## Step 4 — Go on a walk and collect photos

Now switch roles: you are a participant. Put your phone in your pocket and go for a 15-minute walk — on campus, in your neighbourhood, wherever fits the brief you wrote.

When something catches your attention according to your brief:

1. Open the Telegram bot conversation on your phone.
2. Send `/photo` to activate photo mode.
3. Take or select a photo from your camera roll and send it.
4. The bot confirms receipt. You can send as many photos as you like — there is no need to open the bot each time after the first.
5. Optionally: after a photo, send a brief `/diary` note explaining what caught your attention and why you photographed it. This context will be valuable during analysis.

Aim for 10–20 images. Quantity matters less than variety: photograph things that are different from each other rather than multiple versions of the same moment.

{: .note }
> Resist editing yourself too strictly on the walk. Photograph what the brief leads you to notice, including things that do not yet fit neatly into a pattern. Some of the most analytically interesting images will be the ones you are least sure about.

---

## Step 5 — Verify collection in Data Foundry

Back at your laptop:

1. Go to your `Walk Photos` Media Dataset in Data Foundry and click **Data Table**.
2. You should see one row per photo, with a timestamp and a thumbnail preview. Count the images: does the number match what you sent?
3. If any images are missing, check whether they were sent as documents rather than photos in Telegram — the bot only processes native photo messages.

Take a first look before doing anything else. Scroll through the thumbnails without analysing. Note any immediate first impressions: What variety did you produce? What patterns are already visible? What surprises you about the collection as a whole?

---

## Step 6 — Anonymise first

Before any further analysis, review every image for content that should not be visible to others.

For each image, check for:
- **Faces** — blur or replace with a solid shape. A light blur may allow re-identification; pixelation or a coloured rectangle is more robust.
- **Identifiers** — licence plates, screens, branded clothing, documents, and anything that identifies a specific person or location unintentionally.
- **Metadata** — images carry embedded EXIF data (timestamps, GPS coordinates, device identifiers). Strip this before sharing or uploading to any shared system.

Face blurring can be done manually in any photo editor, or automatically using a face-detection script applied to the images you download from Data Foundry. Automated tools are faster but imperfect — always review the output manually, especially for faces at angles, in profile, or in poor lighting.

{: .note }
> If a photograph cannot be adequately anonymised without destroying what makes it analytically interesting, decide whether to exclude it from shared analysis or to describe it in text instead. Document every such decision and your reasoning.

Re-upload anonymised versions to the `Walk Photos` dataset, or flag the originals as requiring restricted access.

## Step 7 — Browse and create a visual log

The visual equivalent of plotting a time-series is simply to watch — or in this case, to browse. Before reaching for any theme or pattern, look at all the images in sequence.

As you browse, create a **visual log** in your `Walk Log` Annotation Dataset: for each image that strikes you, add an annotation with:
- The image reference (or timestamp)
- A brief descriptive note — what is in the image, what moment it captures
- One observation: what surprised you, what raised a question, or what you do not fully understand

Keep notes descriptive at this stage. Resist interpretation: write *"a motion sensor above the entrance, angled to cover the full doorway"* rather than *"invasive surveillance"*. The log does not interpret; it notices.

After your first pass, read back through the log. Which moments appear in multiple images? Which single image feels most important and why? Which images do you find yourself glossing over?

---

## Step 8 — Thematic compilation

Now look across the log for candidate patterns: recurring types of moment, or a tension that appears in several different images.

Select 3–5 images that seem to illustrate a single candidate theme — not because they show the same thing, but because they seem to share an underlying idea. A theme is not a topic label (`wayfinding`) but an interpretive claim (`wayfinding cues are invisible until they fail`).

In your `Walk Log` Annotation Dataset, create a **theme entry** that includes:
- A provisional theme name and a one-sentence claim
- References to the 3–5 images in the compilation
- A note on what holds the images together — and what, if you are honest, does not quite fit

This compilation is provisional. It may be revised substantially once you bring it to participants or team members. Its purpose right now is to give the candidate theme a concrete, shareable form.

---

## Step 9 — Reflect on positionality

Return to your collection brief and your log. Answer the following questions in a short written note (a few sentences each):

1. **What did the brief invite?** Re-read the message you sent yourself in Step 3. What kinds of moments does it select for? What does it make invisible?
2. **What did you actually photograph?** Compare the images against the brief. Where did your camera go that the brief did not anticipate? What did you pass without photographing?
3. **What does your selection reveal about your analytical lens?** Which images feel significant to you, and why? Are there patterns in what you found worth capturing that say something about your assumptions or interests?

This reflexivity note is not a confession of bias. It is part of your analytical record — it documents what the exploration could and could not find, which is essential for others to interpret what you produce.

---

## Step 10 — Prepare for collaborative analysis

The material you have produced — the anonymised images, the log, the thematic compilation, and the reflexivity note — is ready to bring into a participatory session.

Before Chapter 5:
- Export the 3–5 thematic compilation images for use in a Video Card Game or image-sorting session
- Frame your candidate theme as a question, not a claim: *"Does this pattern reflect your experience too?"*
- Note one moment from your log that you cannot explain — something you photographed but whose significance you do not understand — as an opening for participant interpretation

{: .note }
> The textbook (p. 329) describes thematic clips as *designer clay* — material that can be arranged, recomposed, and shown in different configurations as the analytical question evolves. Your compilation is not a finding; it is a starting point for conversation.
