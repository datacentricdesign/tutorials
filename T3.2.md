## Introduction

In this tutorial you will design and run a small data donation campaign. You will invite classmates to donate their **ChatGPT or Claude.ai conversation history**—data they already own and can export under GDPR data portability rights—and build a simple pipeline to collect and profile what they share.

Rather than building something elaborate, you will build something honest: a minimal, consent-first campaign where donors understand exactly what they are sharing and why. Your analysis will focus on **metadata**—structural patterns in how, when, and how much people use AI tools—leaving message content untouched.

By the end you will have:
- A live data donation campaign hosted on Data Foundry, with a secure upload link for each participant
- A processing notebook that turns raw exports into a structured metadata dataset
- A cross-donor profile of AI tool usage patterns

{: .note }
> A key piece of advice from the Athlete case study (DCD textbook chapter 12): *"Be a pilot participant of your own study if you can! This is a good starting point to see what the data looks like and to empathise with what participants may experience."* Follow this advice — export and donate your own data first, before asking anyone else.

## Before you start

### Export your own conversation data

**Claude.ai:**
1. Log in and go to **Settings → Privacy → Export data**.
2. Download and unzip the archive. The conversation file is typically named `conversations.json`.

Keep the unzipped folder to hand — you will load it in Step 1.

### Set up your Data Foundry project

4. In the **General** tab, add an **IoT Dataset** — this will store the processed metadata records. Name it `AI Usage Metadata`. Note its numeric **dataset ID** from the top-left corner.
5. In the **Sources** tab, add a device called `donation-pipeline`. Note its **source ID**.
6. Go to **Data tools → Notebooks**, click **Add a New Notebook**, choose your project and the `Donation Notebook` dataset, and name it `Donation Pipeline`.

## Step 2 — Extract metadata

You will extract only **structural metadata** from the conversations — no message content. This reflects the data minimisation principle and models responsible practice: you can reveal patterns in AI tool usage without reading anyone's messages.

```python
import pandas as pd
from datetime import datetime, timezone


def extract_metadata(conversations, donor_id):
    """
    Convert a list of conversation objects into a flat list of metadata rows.
    One row per conversation. No message content is stored.

    donor_id: an anonymised identifier you assign to this donor (e.g. "D01")
    """
    rows = []
    for conv in conversations:
        # Collect all messages from the mapping dictionary
        messages = [
            node["message"]
            for node in conv.get("mapping", {}).values()
            if node.get("message") is not None
        ]

        # Count messages by author role
        role_counts = {}
        for msg in messages:
            role = msg.get("author", {}).get("role", "unknown")
            role_counts[role] = role_counts.get(role, 0) + 1

        rows.append({
            "donor_id":            donor_id,
            "conv_id":             conv.get("id"),
            "title":               conv.get("title"),       # title only, not content
            "created_at":          conv.get("create_time"), # Unix timestamp (float)
            "updated_at":          conv.get("update_time"),
            "n_messages":          len(messages),
            "n_user_turns":        role_counts.get("user", 0),
            "n_assistant_turns":   role_counts.get("assistant", 0),
            "n_tool_turns":        role_counts.get("tool", 0),
            "collected_at":        datetime.now(timezone.utc).isoformat(),
        })

    return rows


# Run on your own data first — you are donor D00, the pilot participant
rows = extract_metadata(conversations, donor_id="D00")
df = pd.DataFrame(rows)

print(df.shape)
df.head()
```

### Convert timestamps

The `create_time` field is a Unix timestamp (seconds since 1 January 1970 UTC). Convert it to a readable datetime:

```python
# Convert Unix timestamps to datetime objects
df["created_dt"]  = pd.to_datetime(df["created_at"], unit="s", utc=True)
df["updated_dt"]  = pd.to_datetime(df["updated_at"], unit="s", utc=True)

# Extract the date only — useful for grouping by day or month later
df["created_date"] = df["created_dt"].dt.date

print(df[["donor_id", "title", "created_date", "n_messages"]].head(10))
```

## Step 3 — Design the campaign

Before inviting anyone, write the three documents that define any data donation campaign.

### 3a. Purpose statement

Write one clear paragraph describing your study. It should answer:

- Who is conducting the research, and for what purpose?
- What data is being requested, and — crucially — what will you **not** collect or analyse?
- How will the data be stored, for how long, and who will have access?

Keep it specific. Vague purpose statements reduce trust and willingness to participate. A sentence like *"your data will help us understand AI usage patterns"* is less informative than *"we will analyse conversation counts, lengths, and timestamps — not message content — to map how students use AI tools over the semester"*.

### 3b. Export instructions for participants

Participants need step-by-step instructions to export their data from ChatGPT or Claude.ai. Write these instructions based on what you did yourself in *Before you start*. Include screenshots where possible. Confirm the steps still work immediately before distributing them, as platform interfaces change.

### 3c. Consent form

Draft a short consent form covering:

1. What data the participant is sharing (and what you will not process)
2. How the data will be stored and analysed
3. Their right to withdraw and have their data deleted at any time
4. Whether the data will appear in any published or presented output

> The Athlete case study used **iterative consent**: donors were invited to reassess their consent at each stage, as they gained better understanding of what their data contained. Consider whether you would do the same — for example, by sending a follow-up message once you have processed a donation, summarising what you can see.

### 3d. Add participants in Data Foundry

1. Go to the **Participants** tab of your project.
2. Add each participant by email address. Data Foundry will generate a secure, personalised upload link for the `Raw Donations` dataset.
3. Send each participant your purpose statement, export instructions, and consent form **before** sharing their upload link. The upload link should come after they have had a chance to read and agree to the consent form.

{: .note }
> Participants outside the EU can still request a copy of their data from ChatGPT or Claude.ai — data portability under the GDPR applies to EU residents, but both platforms offer exports to all users as a product feature.

## Step 4 — Process a donation

When a participant uploads their `conversations.json` to the `Raw Donations` dataset, you process it in your notebook. This step handles one donated file.

```python
import json
import pandas as pd
from datetime import datetime, timezone

# Replace the values below with a specific donation
DONATED_FILE = "conversations.json"   # path to the uploaded file
DONOR_ID     = "D01"                  # anonymised ID — keep your ID-to-email mapping private

with open(DONATED_FILE, "r", encoding="utf-8") as f:
    donated_conversations = json.load(f)

print(f"Donor {DONOR_ID}: {len(donated_conversations)} conversations")

# Reuse the extraction function from Step 2
rows_donor = extract_metadata(donated_conversations, donor_id=DONOR_ID)
df_donor   = pd.DataFrame(rows_donor)
df_donor["created_dt"]   = pd.to_datetime(df_donor["created_at"], unit="s", utc=True)
df_donor["created_date"] = df_donor["created_dt"].dt.date

print(df_donor.shape)
df_donor.head()
```

### Push to Data Foundry

{: .note }
> The dataset token is a base64 string that often ends with `=`. Because it is embedded in the URL path, that `=` must be replaced with `%3D` — otherwise the server returns a 404. The line `DATASET_TOKEN.replace("=", "%3D")` handles this automatically.

```python
from pyodide.http import pyfetch  # use requests.post() if running outside Starboard

DF_BASE       = "https://datafoundry.io.tudelft.nl"
SOURCE_ID     = "paste-your-source-id"      # the 'donation-pipeline' device ID
DATASET_TOKEN = "paste-your-dataset-token"  # IoT dataset token
DATASET_ID    = 123                          # replace with your IoT dataset ID

for _, row in df_donor.iterrows():
    record = {
        "source_id": SOURCE_ID,
        "activity":  "conversation",
        "data": json.dumps({
            "donor_id":           row["donor_id"],
            "conv_id":            row["conv_id"],
            "title":              row["title"],
            "created_at":         row["created_at"],
            "updated_at":         row["updated_at"],
            "n_messages":         int(row["n_messages"]),
            "n_user_turns":       int(row["n_user_turns"]),
            "n_assistant_turns":  int(row["n_assistant_turns"]),
            "n_tool_turns":       int(row["n_tool_turns"]),
        })
    }

    resp = await pyfetch(
        f"{DF_BASE}/api/v1/datasets/ts/{DATASET_ID}/{DATASET_TOKEN.replace('=', '%3D')}",
        method="POST",
        headers={"Content-Type": "application/json"},
        body=json.dumps(record)
    )
    print(f"Posted '{str(row['title'])[:40]}' — status {resp.status}")
```

A successful run will print a line like the following for each conversation. Status `200` confirms the record was stored:

```
Posted 'How do neural networks learn?' — status 200
Posted 'Python list comprehension examples' — status 200
Posted 'Help with regression analysis' — status 200
```

## Step 5 — Profile the dataset

Once you have processed several donations, run a cross-donor profile. For now, use your own pilot data.

```python
# In practice, concatenate rows from all donors:
# df_all = pd.concat([df_d00, df_d01, df_d02, ...], ignore_index=True)
df_all = df_donor.copy()

# --- Overview ---
print("Donors:", df_all["donor_id"].nunique())
print("Conversations:", len(df_all))
print("Messages (total):", df_all["n_messages"].sum())

# --- Conversation length ---
print("\nConversation length (messages):")
print(df_all["n_messages"].describe().round(1))

# --- Usage over time ---
monthly = (
    df_all
    .groupby(df_all["created_dt"].dt.to_period("M"))
    .size()
    .rename("conversations")
)
print("\nConversations per month:")
print(monthly)

# --- Tool use ---
# Conversations with at least one tool call indicate use of plugins, code interpreter, etc.
tool_users = (df_all["n_tool_turns"] > 0).sum()
print(f"\nConversations with tool calls: {tool_users} ({100 * tool_users / len(df_all):.0f}%)")

# --- Donor comparison (once you have multiple donors) ---
print("\nPer-donor summary:")
print(df_all.groupby("donor_id").agg(
    conversations    = ("conv_id",      "count"),
    total_messages   = ("n_messages",   "sum"),
    mean_length      = ("n_messages",   "mean"),
    first_use        = ("created_date", "min"),
    last_use         = ("created_date", "max"),
).round(1))
```

## Reflect

Before using this data in a design project, consider:

1. **What metadata reveals.** You collected no message content — yet the dataset holds conversation counts, timestamps, and turn-counts per donor. What could someone infer about a person from these fields alone? Does "metadata only" mean "private"?

2. **The platform shapes the data.** ChatGPT and Claude.ai exports have different formats and may cover different time ranges depending on when each donor started using the service. Some donors may have used both tools, or switched platforms partway through. How does this affect comparability across donors?

3. **Iterative consent.** The Athlete case study invited donors to reassess their consent after exploring their own data, because *"at the time of donation, donors often don't know what they don't know"* about it. How would you apply this to AI conversation data — especially for donors who have never seen their own export before?

4. **Value exchange.** What do participants gain by donating their AI usage data to your study? How does this differ from donating fitness data, and how might it affect who is willing to participate and who is not?

5. **Selective donation.** The Athlete case study let donors choose which data types to share and redact individual data points before uploading. What equivalent choices would be meaningful for conversation history — and how would partial or incomplete datasets affect your analysis?
