# T5.2 · Visual and Multimodal Data Analysis

**Location:** `textbook/5/analytics.tex:142–456`
**Textbook context:** Collaborative video analysis, multimodal triangulation, annotation as sensemaking, manual vs automated analysis, privacy and inference.
**Data Foundry features:** Annotation Dataset
**Platform pages:** [Annotation Dataset](https://data-foundry.net/Learning/Datasets/DatasetsOverview/annotationdataset.html)

---

## Introduction

Attaching a GoPro to an object and letting it record in use is a lightweight form of **object ethnography**: the camera becomes the object's perspective on the world, and the embedded telemetry becomes a continuous record of the object's physical experience. A bicycle ridden through a city, a wheelchair navigating a building, a shopping trolley moving through a supermarket, a hand tool used on a worksite — any object that moves, vibrates, or responds to its environment can be instrumented this way with off-the-shelf hardware. The technique scales from a quick design probe to a structured research study, and it produces exactly the kind of multimodal data the textbook analyses in the context of triangulation and collaborative sense-making.

This tutorial uses a dataset of 15 bike rides as a shared worked example. The bicycle is the object under investigation: the GoPro captures what it saw and what it felt. Your task here is not to become experts in cycling but to learn a method you can immediately replicate on whatever object is relevant to your own design project — by taping a GoPro to it and recording it in use.

In this tutorial you will move between video and sensor signals, annotate moments of interest, and prepare material for a collaborative interpretation session. Along the way you will encounter the core tension the textbook identifies — what humans see that algorithms miss, and vice versa — not as an abstract claim but as something you will directly experience.

By the end you will have:
- Hands-on experience triangulating between video and telemetry data
- A set of annotations in your Data Foundry Annotation Dataset
- A short thematic compilation ready for a collaborative Video Card Game session
- A written reflection on what each modality contributed and what neither could explain

## Before you start

### Download the shared dataset

1. Open the shared OneDrive folder your instructor has provided and download the entire `bike_rides` folder to your laptop. The folder contains 15 subfolders, one per ride:

```
bike_rides/
  ride_01/
    video.mp4        ← GoPro footage
    telemetry.csv    ← TIMESTAMP, ACCL_x/y/z, GYRO_x/y/z
  ride_02/
    ...
```

2. Note the total size before downloading. The videos are large; make sure you have sufficient disk space and a stable connection.

### Set up your Data Foundry project

3. Go to [Data Foundry](https://datafoundry.io.tudelft.nl/) and open your existing project, or create a new one named `Bike Rides Analysis`.
4. In the **General** tab, add an **Annotation Dataset**. Name it `Ride Annotations`.

### Open the multimodal viewer

5. Open [viewer.html](resources/T5.2/viewer.html) in **Chrome or Edge**. The viewer reads video and telemetry files directly from your local folder using the browser's File System Access API, which allows a web page to open local files without a server. Chrome and Edge support this API; Firefox and Safari do not. The file is in the tutorial resources folder and runs entirely in your browser with no installation required.
6. Click **Open folder** and select either one `ride_XX` subfolder or the entire `bike_rides` parent folder. If you open the parent folder a ride selector will appear in the header.
7. Verify that scrubbing the video moves the red cursor on both telemetry charts, and that clicking a point on either chart seeks the video to that moment.

{: .note }
> If you are on a managed device that restricts filesystem permissions, contact your instructor.

## Step 1 — First encounter: watch and hypothesise

Before opening any sensor data, watch three rides using only the video.

As you watch each ride, write three observations:

- Something you **noticed** — a moment that caught your attention
- Something that **surprised** you — something you did not expect
- Something you **could not explain** — a moment whose significance you do not understand

After watching all three, write a brief **context hypothesis** for each ride (3–4 sentences): what terrain and conditions you think were involved, what kind of rider you imagine, what seemed to be happening. Write it as a hypothesis, not a fact.

Keep both sets of notes. In Step 4 you will return to them and ask: what did the telemetry confirm, contradict, or leave unresolved — and what did neither modality explain?

{: .note }
> Use the **Video only** toggle in the viewer header to hide the telemetry charts while you watch. The point of this step is to establish what video alone reveals — and what questions it leaves open — before the sensor data influences your reading.

---

## Step 2 — Enter the telemetry

Open the multimodal viewer for one of the three rides you just watched.

Spend 10 minutes exploring the telemetry without a specific question in mind. Look for:

- **Spikes** — sudden large values in the accelerometer or gyroscope
- **Plateaus** — sustained periods of low or high signal
- **Transitions** — moments where the signal character changes

When you find a feature that interests you, click it in the chart to jump the video to that timestamp. Watch what was happening.

Then try the reverse: find a moment in the video that you remember from Step 1 — one of your "noticed" or "could not explain" observations — and locate the corresponding moment in the telemetry chart. What does the sensor show at that instant?

Write a short note (3–5 sentences) comparing what each modality contributed for at least two moments. The note does not need to reach a conclusion — it just needs to record what you saw.

{: .note }
> You are replicating a pattern observed in design research sessions with multimodal data: some people start from a feature in the sensor signal and look for the event in the video; others start from a moment in the video and look for it in the data. Both are valid entry points. Which felt more natural to you, and why?

## Step 3 — Annotate moments of interest

Open the multimodal viewer for two activities (they can include the one from Step 2). Annotate the first activity, then use the activity selector in the header to switch to the second — your annotations are preserved when you switch, and the list below the form shows all annotations colour-coded by activity. For each activity, add **4–6 annotations**.

For each annotation, record:

| Field | What to write |
|---|---|
| Activity | e.g. `ride_03` |
| Timestamp | Seconds from the start of the video (auto-filled from the current position) |
| Signal | Which sensor channel drew your attention, if any |
| Description | What is happening — descriptive, not interpretive |
| Interpretation | What this might mean for a design question |
| Gap | What neither the video nor the sensor can tell you |

The **Gap** field is the most important. It is where you document the limit of the data — the question that would require talking to the rider, or collecting additional data, to answer.

When you finish a session, click **Export CSV** in the viewer. Save the downloaded `annotations.csv` file into the `bike_rides` folder (the parent folder, not inside a ride subfolder). The next time you open that folder, the viewer will automatically reload all your annotations so you can continue where you left off.

{: .note }
> Keep descriptions and interpretations separate. Write *"the bike decelerates sharply and the rider steers left onto a footpath"* rather than *"the rider felt unsafe on the road."* The description is what the data shows; the interpretation is your reading of it. Conflating them obscures both.

---

## Step 4 — Triangulation

Return to your Step 1 notes and your Step 3 annotations. For each of the following, write a short paragraph (4–6 sentences):

**A moment where video and telemetry agree.** Describe what each modality shows and explain what their agreement adds — why is convergence across two sources more informative than either alone?

**A moment where video and telemetry diverge — or where one is silent.** Describe the discrepancy. What explanation could account for it? What additional data or context would resolve it?

**A moment from your "could not explain" list that the telemetry helped clarify** — and one that the telemetry made no clearer.

**Your context hypotheses from Step 1, revisited.** For one ride, compare what you wrote before seeing the telemetry with what you now think is happening. What did the sensor data confirm? What did it contradict? What does neither modality settle — and what would require asking the rider directly?

These paragraphs are the analytical output of your individual exploration and your preparation for the collaborative session: bring them as your interpretive position, ready to be challenged.

---

## Step 5 — Video Card Game preparation

Look across all three activities you have explored. Select **4–6 short clips** (15–60 seconds each) that seem to share an underlying analytical theme — not a topic label (`rough terrain`) but an interpretive claim (`physical disruption from road surface creates moments where sensor data and rider experience diverge`).

A theme is an argument, not a category. The clips should be evidence for that argument.

### Building the compilation in the viewer

The viewer's **Clips** section (below the annotation list in the left panel) lets you define and export your compilation:

1. Navigate to the moment where you want a clip to begin. Click **Set to current** next to the **Start** field to record that timestamp.
2. Navigate to the end of the clip. Click **Set to current** next to the **End** field.
3. Enter a short, descriptive **Name** for the clip (e.g. `Junction braking — sensor spike without visual warning`).
4. Click **Add clip**. The clip appears in the list below with its activity, time range, and duration.
5. Repeat across the activities in your compilation. Clips from different activities are saved together.
6. When you have 4–6 clips, click **Export PDF**.

The PDF contains one page per clip: the clip name, a video frame from the midpoint of the clip, and the accelerometer and gyroscope charts zoomed to that time window. Bring the PDF to the collaborative session — it can be shown on screen or printed.

{: .note }
> The video snapshot and telemetry charts are captured at the moment you click **Add clip**, while the activity is loaded. You can switch to other activities and add more clips before exporting — the PDF will be complete regardless of which activity is loaded when you click Export PDF.

### Document your theme

In your `Ride Annotations` Annotation Dataset in Data Foundry, create a **theme entry** containing:

- A provisional theme name and a one-sentence claim
- References to the clips in your compilation (activity + timestamp range)
- A note on what holds the clips together — and, honestly, what does not quite fit
- One question to put to the group: *"Does this pattern hold across activities you explored that I did not?"*

---

## Step 6 — Collaborative Video Card Game session (in class)

Run a **20–30 minute session** with 2–4 peers. Each person brings their compilation and theme claim.

For each compilation:

1. The presenter shows the clips (in the multimodal viewer, so the group can see both video and telemetry) and states their theme claim.
2. The group discusses: Does the claim hold? What do others see in the clips? Are there clips from other rides that would strengthen or undermine the theme?
3. Record: which themes the group agreed on, which were contested, and — critically — **one moment where two people read the same clip differently**.

The disagreements are not failures. In the textbook's framing, they surface the implicit assumptions each viewer brought. Document them as findings, not problems.

{: .note }
> If someone in the group explored rides you did not, ask them to show you a moment from their annotations. The point of a collaborative session is that the group has seen more material than any individual — and sees it through different lenses.

---

## Step 7 — Reflect: what did each modality contribute?

After the collaborative session, write a short reflection (one paragraph per question):

1. **What did the video reveal that the sensor data could not?** Think about context, intention, and meaning — the things the textbook calls "why something happened, not just what."

2. **What did the telemetry reveal that the video could not?** Think about consistency, scale, and pattern — things that are invisible to the eye but systematic in the signal.

3. **What did collaborative analysis reveal that your solo analysis did not?** Where did another person's reading change or extend your interpretation?

4. **What could be inferred from this data that a rider might not expect?** The accelerometer and GPS together can reveal route regularity, time of day, and riding style. Could any of those inferences be sensitive? What would responsible use of this data look like?

## Optional — Object detection

Apply a pre-trained YOLO model (via Python) to 20 frames sampled from one ride. Compare what the algorithm detects with what your video log noted for the same moments.

Manually annotate the same 20 frames in your Annotation Dataset as a ground truth. Compute precision and recall of the automated detection against your manual labels.

Then ask: what does the object detector infer beyond what is directly visible — and beyond what the rider consented to share? A detected person, a road sign, or a medical facility in the background can reveal information that was never the focus of collection. Document your assessment as a short privacy audit.
