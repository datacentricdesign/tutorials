## Introduction

T4.3 produced candidate themes from close reading of 13 wheelchair ethics assessments. The themes were grounded in specific text — you could point to the quotations that anchored each one. But they were also local: built from one product, one sub-corpus, 13 voices.

This tutorial asks whether they hold. You will scale from 13 documents to 106, from one product to seven, and from reading to computation. Two methods extend the reach of your qualitative analysis: **sentiment analysis** to characterise the emotional register of privacy writing across the corpus, and **topic modelling** to discover whether the corpus organises itself by product (wheelchair responses cluster together, hotel responses cluster together) or by concept (concerns about data storage cluster together regardless of product). Neither method replaces the qualitative reading you did in T4.3 — they stress-test it, extend it, and surface patterns that close reading of a sample could not reach.

This tutorial also marks a tool change. You are moving from Starboard (Python in the browser) to a local Python environment. The browser-based environment suits quick exploration; for text analytics requiring full NLP libraries, a local setup is more capable and closer to how you will work in practice.

By the end you will have:
- A working local Python environment with the libraries needed for text analytics
- A sentiment profile of the full Privacy column across all seven connected products
- Topic models at two resolutions (k=5 and k=10) with an interpretation of what they reveal
- A test of your T4.3 themes against the full corpus
- A completed RTA analysis: reviewed themes (phase 4), defined themes (phase 5), and a written synthesis (phase 6)

## Step 2 — Preprocessing: what the algorithm sees

Before any algorithm runs, the text must be transformed into a numerical form. Every transformation discards information. Understanding what is lost is as important as understanding what is preserved.

### Show the before and after

```python
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import CountVectorizer

sample_text = priv["Privacy"].iloc[2]   # pick any response

# What sklearn's tokenizer produces with stop word removal
analyser = CountVectorizer(lowercase=True, stop_words="english").build_analyzer()
tokens = analyser(sample_text)

print("ORIGINAL (first 400 characters):")
print(sample_text[:400])
print(f"\nTOKENS ({len(tokens)} total):")
print(" | ".join(tokens[:30]), "...")
```

Read the original and the token list side by side. Then answer these questions in a markdown cell:

1. Which words disappeared in tokenisation, and which of those carry meaning in an ethics context? Look specifically for negation words such as `not`, `never`, `without`.
2. Are there multi-word phrases that tokenisation splits into parts — phrases where the whole means something different from the parts (e.g., `third party`, `informed consent`)?
3. The vectoriser treats `camera` and `cameras` as different tokens. Would that matter for this corpus?

{: .note }
> The `ngram_range=(1, 2)` parameter used in Steps 3 and 4 partially addresses point 2 by including two-word sequences as features. But it cannot recover lost negation.

{: .note }
> You may notice a token like `äôs` in the topic model output later. This is a tokenisation artifact: the smart quote character `'` (U+2019) in phrases like *user's* or *company's* was not recognised as punctuation and survived as a fragment. It carries no meaning and can be ignored — but its presence is a reminder that real-world text is messier than preprocessing pipelines assume, and that inspecting raw token lists before running models is worth the time.

## Step 3 — Sentiment analysis with VADER

VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner) assigns a compound sentiment score between −1 (very negative) and +1 (very positive) to each text. It was designed for social media — short, informal posts with clear emotional language.

Ethics writing is neither short nor informal. Running VADER on it is therefore deliberately instructive: the mismatch between the algorithm's output and your reading of the same texts is the lesson.

### Score the corpus

```python
from vaderSentiment.vaderSentiment import SentimentIntensityAnalyzer

analyser = SentimentIntensityAnalyzer()
priv["vader"] = priv["Privacy"].apply(
    lambda x: analyser.polarity_scores(x)["compound"]
)

print("Sentiment distribution:")
print(priv["vader"].describe().round(3))
print(f"\nPositive (>0.05):  {(priv['vader'] >  0.05).sum()}")
print(f"Neutral  (±0.05):  {(priv['vader'].abs() <= 0.05).sum()}")
print(f"Negative (<-0.05): {(priv['vader'] < -0.05).sum()}")
```

### Visualise sentiment across products

```python
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(13, 4))

# Overall histogram
axes[0].hist(priv["vader"], bins=25, color="steelblue", edgecolor="white")
axes[0].axvline(0, color="crimson", ls="--", lw=1.2, label="neutral")
axes[0].set_xlabel("VADER compound score")
axes[0].set_ylabel("Count")
axes[0].set_title("Sentiment distribution — all privacy responses")
axes[0].legend()

# Mean sentiment by product
product_sent = priv.groupby("Product")["vader"].mean().sort_values()
colors = ["crimson" if v < 0 else "steelblue" for v in product_sent.values]
axes[1].barh(product_sent.index, product_sent.values, color=colors)
axes[1].axvline(0, color="black", lw=0.8)
axes[1].set_xlabel("Mean VADER compound score")
axes[1].set_title("Mean sentiment by product")

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

## Step 4 — Topic modelling with NMF

Non-negative Matrix Factorisation (NMF) discovers clusters of words that tend to co-occur across documents, suggesting underlying topics. Unlike reading, it operates on the full corpus at once. Unlike clustering, it allows documents to have mixed membership across topics — a response can be partially about data storage and partially about caregiver access.

### Fit the model

```python
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer
from sklearn.decomposition import NMF

def fit_nmf(texts, n_topics):
    vec = TfidfVectorizer(
        lowercase=True,
        stop_words="english",
        min_df=2,           # ignore terms in only one document
        max_df=0.85,        # ignore terms in >85% of documents (near-universal words)
        ngram_range=(1, 2), # include two-word phrases
    )
    tfidf = vec.fit_transform(texts)
    model = NMF(n_components=n_topics, random_state=42, max_iter=500)
    W = model.fit_transform(tfidf)   # document × topic weights
    return vec, model, W

def show_topics(model, vectorizer, n_words=10):
    terms = vectorizer.get_feature_names_out()
    for i, row in enumerate(model.components_):
        top = [terms[j] for j in row.argsort()[:-n_words - 1:-1]]
        print(f"  Topic {i:2d}: {', '.join(top)}")
```

### Run at k=5

```python
vec5, nmf5, W5 = fit_nmf(priv["Privacy"], n_topics=5)

print("k=5 topics — top words:")
show_topics(nmf5, vec5)
```

Read the top words for each topic and try to name it. A topic is not a list of words — it is an interpretive claim about what those words, together, represent. Write your five names in a markdown cell.

### Run at k=10

```python
vec10, nmf10, W10 = fit_nmf(priv["Privacy"], n_topics=10)

print("k=10 topics — top words:")
show_topics(nmf10, vec10)
```

At k=10 some k=5 topics will split into more specific sub-topics. Others will remain stable. Note which split and which held — instability often signals that the k=5 topic was conflating two different ideas.

### Do topics follow products or concepts?

This is the central analytical question. Assign each document its dominant topic and cross-tabulate with product.

```python
priv["topic5"]  = W5.argmax(axis=1)
priv["topic10"] = W10.argmax(axis=1)

def topic_product_heatmap(priv, topic_col, n_topics, title):
    xtab = pd.crosstab(priv[topic_col], priv["Product"])
    # normalise by product: % of each product's responses in each topic
    xtab_pct = xtab.div(xtab.sum(axis=0), axis=1)

    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(11, 0.7 * n_topics + 1.5))
    im = ax.imshow(xtab_pct.values, aspect="auto", cmap="YlOrRd", vmin=0, vmax=1)
    ax.set_xticks(range(len(xtab_pct.columns)))
    ax.set_xticklabels(xtab_pct.columns, rotation=30, ha="right", fontsize=9)
    ax.set_yticks(range(n_topics))
    ax.set_yticklabels([f"Topic {i}" for i in range(n_topics)], fontsize=9)
    ax.set_title(title)
    plt.colorbar(im, ax=ax, label="Share of product's responses", fraction=0.02, pad=0.02)
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

topic_product_heatmap(priv, "topic5",  5,  "k=5  — topic share by product")
topic_product_heatmap(priv, "topic10", 10, "k=10 — topic share by product")
```

Interpret each heatmap. Two extreme patterns are possible:

- **Product-aligned topics**: one product dominates one topic (a dark row concentrated in one or two columns). This would mean the NMF has picked up product-specific vocabulary more than shared ethical concepts.
- **Concept-aligned topics**: responses from multiple products are distributed across a topic. This would mean the model found cross-product conceptual structure — evidence that "privacy" organises itself into distinct sub-concepts that cut across product categories.

In a markdown cell, describe what you see. Is the pattern closer to product-aligned or concept-aligned? Are there any topics that are clearly one or the other?

## Step 5 — Test your T4.3 themes

Your T4.3 candidate themes were built from the wheelchair sub-corpus. Do they hold across all products and the full Privacy column?

### Keyword search across the corpus

For each candidate theme, define 2–3 keywords that capture its core claim. The search is approximate — it catches responses where those words appear, not where the theme is expressed.

```python
# Replace with your own themes and keywords from T4.3
MY_THEMES = {
    "Your first theme name": ["keyword1", "keyword2"],
    "Your second theme name": ["keyword3", "keyword4"],
    "Your third theme name": ["keyword5", "keyword6"],
}

for theme, keywords in MY_THEMES.items():
    pattern = r'\b(?:' + '|'.join(keywords) + r')\b'
    hits = priv[priv["Privacy"].str.contains(pattern, case=False, na=False, regex=True)]

    print(f"\n'{theme}': {len(hits)}/{len(priv)} documents match")
    print(f"  Products: {hits['Product'].value_counts().to_dict()}")
    for _, row in hits.head(2).iterrows():
        excerpt = " ".join(row["Privacy"].split()[:40])
        print(f"  [{row['Product']}] {excerpt}...")
```

For each theme, note in a markdown cell:

1. **Coverage**: Does the theme appear broadly across products, or mainly in the wheelchair responses?
2. **Surprise**: Which product's responses pick up the theme in a way you did not expect?
3. **Absence**: Are there products where you expected the theme but did not find it?

## Step 6 — RTA phases 4–6

The computational results are evidence to think with, not findings to report. Phases 4–6 of Reflexive Thematic Analysis bring the qualitative and computational accounts into dialogue.

### Phase 4: Review and revise themes

Return to Atlas.ti with the computational results visible alongside it. For each candidate theme:

- Read through the quotations you coded under this theme. Does each quotation genuinely express the theme's core claim, or did you include it because it was close enough?
- Check the keyword search results from Step 5. Are the documents that match your keywords the same ones you would have coded in Atlas.ti? Where do they diverge, and why?
- Check whether the theme corresponds to an NMF topic. If not, is the theme too specific to appear computationally, or too diffuse?

Decide for each theme: **keep**, **split**, **merge**, or **dissolve**. A theme that only two or three quotations support may dissolve — or may be the most interesting finding in the dataset, worth holding onto precisely because it is rare.

### Phase 5: Define and name themes

For each surviving theme, write the following into a markdown cell:

```markdown
**Theme: [final name]**

Definition: [One or two sentences stating the interpretive claim.
Not "responses discuss X" but "X is framed as Y because Z."]

Representative quote:
> "[a direct quotation from the corpus that anchors the theme]"

Source: [participant ID and product]

Relation to NMF: [which topic, if any, overlaps with this theme, and where
the computational and qualitative accounts diverge]
```

The name and definition should work together. If the definition is a better name than the name, rename.

### Phase 6: Written synthesis

Write a short synthesis — one paragraph per theme, plus an opening paragraph framing the analytical question and a closing paragraph on what the analysis cannot explain. This is RTA phase 6, and it is part of the analysis, not a summary of it: writing forces the clarity that code editing and close reading do not.

Structure the synthesis as:

1. **Opening**: What question did you bring to this corpus? What method did you use and why?
2. **One paragraph per theme**: State the theme, ground it in a quotation, situate it relative to the NMF results.
3. **Closing**: What does the corpus not capture? Where would you return to participants to verify or challenge what you found?

Keep the synthesis in a markdown cell in your notebook so it stays alongside the code that produced the evidence.
